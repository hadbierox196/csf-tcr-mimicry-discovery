# Code of Conduct

## Why this exists

This project is built by a mixed team — computational researchers, wet-lab
scientists, clinicians, and trainees at different career stages — working
with data derived from real patients facing a serious, often
under-diagnosed neurological condition. The standards below apply
everywhere the team interacts about this work: GitHub (issues, PRs, code
review), lab meetings, Slack/email, and in-person.

## Our standard

We're committed to a harassment-free, respectful environment for everyone,
regardless of career stage, role (wet-lab or dry-lab), institution, gender,
gender identity, sexual orientation, disability, personal appearance, race,
ethnicity, age, religion, or nationality.

Examples of behavior that helps this team work well together:

- Reviewing code and protocols on their merits, and phrasing disagreement
  about a PR or an experimental design as disagreement about the work, not
  the person.
- Being as precise in a bug report or code review as you'd want a lab notebook
  entry to be — specific, reproducible, and generous in assuming good intent.
- Crediting others' contributions accurately, including in authorship
  discussions — see the authorship note in `CONTRIBUTING.md`.
- Respecting that wet-lab and dry-lab work move at different paces and carry
  different constraints (a sample can't be re-run the way a unit test can);
  extend patience across that gap in both directions.
- Treating participant privacy as a personal responsibility, not just a
  policy — the people whose T-cells and tumor sequencing this project
  studies are not in the room, and the standard we hold ourselves to on
  their behalf should reflect that.

Examples of unacceptable behavior:

- Harassment, discriminatory jokes or language, or unwelcome personal
  attention, in any project space.
- Personal attacks, insults, or dismissiveness in code review, issue
  discussion, or lab meetings.
- Sharing another contributor's private communication, data, or unpublished
  results without consent.
- Any attempt to access, export, or discuss patient-derived data outside the
  access controls described in `DATA_GOVERNANCE.md` — this is both a conduct
  violation and, depending on circumstances, a potential regulatory one.
- Misrepresenting authorship or credit for code, protocols, or analyses.

## Scope

Applies to all project spaces (this repository, its issues/PRs/discussions)
and to project-related communication elsewhere (lab meetings, mailing
lists, chat channels) when representing the project.

## Reporting and enforcement

Report concerns to the PI or lab manager designated in `CODEOWNERS`, or, if
that's not appropriate given the nature of the report, to your institution's
standard research-conduct or HR channel. Reports will be handled as
confidentially as possible. Maintainers who violate this Code of Conduct in
their own actions are held to the same standard and may be asked to step
back from a reviewer/maintainer role.

Consequences scale with severity and pattern: a private conversation and
correction for a first, minor issue; a formal warning for repeated or more
serious issues; removal from the project (and, for severe violations,
referral to institutional processes) for the rest.

## Adaptation note

This is a working template, not a legal document — an institution running
this project at scale should have it reviewed against their own HR, Title
IX, and research-conduct policies rather than relying on it as-is. It draws
on the structure of the widely-used Contributor Covenant, adapted here for
a mixed wet-lab/dry-lab research context.
